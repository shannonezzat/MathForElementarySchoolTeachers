  2. If you haven't found one above, can you think of a number that is divisible by 6 and 4 but not divisible by 24?

Try the above with other sets of numbers \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) for example try \(3 \times 5=15\) and \(2 \times 4=8\text{.}\) Do you notice a pattern about the numbers \(a\) and \(b\) where \(a \times b\) is always a factor of numbers where both \(a\) and \(b\) are factors, and when there are numbers that do not have \(a \times b\) as a factor?

In the above example you probably have seen that if \(a\) and \(b\) have no common factors, then \(a \times b\) is necessarily a factor of any number where both \(a\) and \(b\) are factors. Let's try and prove this.

Theorem 6.2.26.

Let \(a,b\) be numbers with no common factors (that is, \(\text{GCD}(a,b)=1\)), and let \(n\) be another number. Then \(ab \mid n \) if and only if \(a \mid n\) and \(b \mid n\text{.}\)
